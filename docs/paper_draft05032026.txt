                   The Miracle on the Han



1    Background
Korea’s Heavy and Chemical Industrialisation. Between 1962 and 1979 South
Korea pursued a state-directed export industrialisation strategy under a series of five-
year plans. The pivot from light to heavy industry began in earnest with President
Park Chung-hee’s Heavy and Chemical Industry (HCI) declaration of 1973, which com-
mitted public investment to six strategic sectors: steel, non-ferrous metals, machinery,
shipbuilding, electronics, and chemicals. Gross fixed capital formation in manufactur-
ing rose from 5 % to over 10 % of GDP within a decade. The structural shift is visible
in the national accounts: manufacturing’s share of GDP rose from around 15 % in
1965 to nearly 30 % by 1985, primarily at the expense of agriculture (Figure 1). This
transformation was among the fastest recorded anywhere in the post-war period.


Industrial parks as the spatial delivery mechanism. Korea’s industrial policy
was implemented primarily through purpose-built industrial complexes (????) — planned
estates with shared infrastructure, tax incentives, and preferential credit for designated
firms. The legal framework traces to the Industrial Sites and Development Act of 1964,
and the first large-scale complex at Guro (Seoul) opened in 1967. During the HCI push,
new parks were established in Changwon (machinery, 1974), Pohang (steel, 1968),
Onsan (non-ferrous metals, 1974), and a cluster of fabricated-metals and electronics
parks along the Gyeongbu motorway corridor. Figure 4 shows the spatial expansion
from 1965 to 1990: the parks were concentrated in the southeastern coastal corridor
and the Seoul–Busan axis, exploiting proximity to deepwater ports and the newly
built highway network. By 1985 eight of the twenty parks recorded in our database
had been established within the fabricated metals sector (industry code 28), the focus
of our SDID analysis.


Roads and market integration. Road construction was both a complement and
a precondition for the industrial parks strategy. The Gyeongbu Expressway (Seoul–
Busan, 428 km) was completed in 1970, followed by five additional motorways by 1981.

Our data show that highway coverage roughly doubled between 1970 and 1980 (Fig-
ure 4). For rural townships, these roads transformed effective distances to urban mar-
kets, enabling both the outward migration of farm labour and the inward penetration
of manufactured inputs and consumer goods.


Geographic concentration of manufacturing. The industrial parks strategy pro-
duced a highly concentrated spatial pattern of manufacturing activity. Figure 3 shows
log production in the fabricated metals sector at the county level in 1968 and 1985,
with active industrial parks overlaid as orange dots. In 1968, before the HCI drive,
production was dispersed with a modest concentration around Seoul. By 1985, it
had shifted decisively towards the southeastern coastal corridor — Changwon, Ulsan,
Pohang, and the Gyeongbu axis — where the major HCI parks were located. The
co-location of high-production counties and park locations is striking and motivates
the county-level SDID analysis in Section 3.1.


Transport infrastructure expansion. The parks were complemented by rapid
road construction. Figure 5 shows paved road and motorway coverage at four points
in time. The Gyeongbu Expressway (Seoul–Busan) opened in 1970, and by 1975
a second arc of motorways had connected Daejeon, Daegu, and the port of Masan.
Paved local road coverage expanded in parallel, with density roughly tripling between
1967 and 1980. This infrastructure build-out reduced travel times to urban markets
for rural townships throughout the country, forming the market access channel that is
the focus of the second part of our empirical analysis.


Structural transformation and rural change. The industrial and infrastructure
push was accompanied by rapid urbanisation and agricultural contraction. Korea’s
urban population share rose from approximately 28 % in 1960 to 57 % by 1980 and
74 % by 1990. The farm household population fell from a peak of roughly 14 million
in the early 1960s to under 6 million by 1990 as workers relocated to industrial cen-
tres. Internal migration flows — dominated by moves from rural provinces to Seoul,
Busan, and the industrial corridor — averaged 4–8 million persons per year through
the 1970s and 1980s. Against this backdrop, understanding whether the gains from
industrialisation reached rural areas — and through what channels — is the central
empirical question of this paper.

2     Empirical Strategy
Our analysis proceeds in two stages that match the two channels through which the
HCI policy affected rural Korea. The first stage asks whether industrial parks actually
raised manufacturing activity in the counties that hosted them — a necessary precondi-
tion for any rural spillover. The second stage estimates how road-driven improvements
in market access propagated these gains to surrounding rural townships.


2.1   Local Manufacturing Effects: Synthetic DiD
To identify the direct effect of hosting an industrial park on county-level manufactur-
ing, we use the Synthetic Difference-in-Differences estimator of ?. SDID simultane-
ously reweights control units (to match each treated county’s pre-treatment outcome
level) and pre-treatment time periods (to downweight periods in which treated and
control trends diverge), providing better pre-treatment fit than either pure synthetic
control or standard two-way fixed effects. The estimator reduces to a standard DiD
when all pre-treatment weights are equal, and to a synthetic control when all period
weights are equal; in our setting the double reweighting exploits both cross-sectional
and time-series structure.
    The sample is the harmonised Mining and Manufacturing Survey (MMS) county-
year panel, 1968–1990, covering 183 counties. The outcome is county-level production
(and employment) in fabricated metals (industry code 28) — the dominant HCI ma-
chinery sector. Treatment is the groundbreaking of an industrial park specialised
in that sector. We identify eight groundbreaking cohorts (1969, 1971, 1972, 1973,
1974, 1977, 1979, 1986), each with a single treated county and 175 donor counties.
Cohort-level SDID estimates are aggregated to a weighted event study using the num-
ber of treated counties per cohort as weights. Standard errors are constructed from a
100-replicate county cluster bootstrap that mirrors the Stata sdid package implemen-
tation. The key identifying assumption is that, absent the park, each treated county’s
trajectory would have continued as reconstructed by the SDID weights; the flat pre-
treatment event-study coefficients reported in Section 3.1 provide direct evidence for
this assumption.

Cohort-level ATT estimates. Table 1 reports cohort-specific ATT estimates. The
largest effects are concentrated in the early HCI cohorts: parks established in 1972 and
1973 raised production by approximately 356,000 and 399,000 MMS units respectively,
and employment by 34,000 and 55,000 workers relative to the synthetic control. These
cohorts correspond to the founding of Changwon (machinery) and the Onsan–Ulsan
complex — the largest industrial estates built during the HCI period. Later cohorts
(1977, 1986) show substantially smaller effects, consistent with diminishing returns
as the best industrial sites were occupied and as the HCI policy was wound down
after 1979. The 1979 cohort, established at the onset of the second oil shock, shows
a near-zero effect for both outcomes, suggesting that macro headwinds substantially
attenuated the local impact for latecomers. Taken together, the SDID estimates con-
firm that Korea’s industrial parks delivered large, targeted manufacturing gains in the
treated counties — the “first stage” of any rural spillover analysis. The subsequent sec-

4     Heterogeneity by Industrial Park Proximity
The interaction estimates and the marginal effects in Figure 7 suggest that the role of
market access depends critically on a township’s position relative to industrial parks.

    The non-farm gradient is striking. The MA coefficient falls from -1.81 in D1 (s.e.
0.80, t = -2.27) to +0.63 in D10 (s.e. 0.31, t = 2.05), with a sign reversal around
the median of the IP-distance distribution. Near industrial parks, road improvements
primarily facilitate worker reallocation into manufacturing. In remote townships, the
same connectivity improvements support local non-farm activity. The service sector
panel reinforces this interpretation: the MA effect on new service establishments is pos-
itive in middle-to-remote deciles (D5–D10) and essentially flat near industrial parks
(D1–D3), consistent with rising rural incomes and population retention — rather than
manufacturing demand — driving local service creation. Farm population follows a
similar middle-distance concentration (strongest retention at D6–D8), where farming
remains the primary livelihood and output market access genuinely raises its returns.
